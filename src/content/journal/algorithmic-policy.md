When a municipality deploys an algorithm to allocate social housing, or a border agency uses predictive scoring to determine admissibility, code has become law — yet without any of the procedural safeguards that democratic societies have spent centuries constructing around legislative power.

This gap between the velocity of algorithmic deployment and the inertia of regulatory frameworks is not merely a technical problem. It is a legitimacy crisis in slow motion.

## The Accountability Deficit

Traditional legal accountability assumes a human decision-maker who can explain their reasoning, be cross-examined, and be held responsible for outcomes. Algorithmic systems fracture this chain. The vendor who built the model, the agency that deployed it, the data broker who supplied training sets — responsibility is distributed across a network that no single accountability mechanism was designed to govern.

## Toward Procedural Safeguards

The most promising regulatory approaches are not those that attempt to audit algorithmic outputs after the fact, but those that mandate substantive procedural requirements upstream: impact assessments before deployment, interpretability requirements embedded in procurement specifications, and formal rights of contestation for affected individuals.
