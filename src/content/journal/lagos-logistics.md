Lagos presents a paradox that urban planners have struggled to resolve for decades: a megacity that functions, at considerable human cost, largely without the formal infrastructure systems that textbooks prescribe as preconditions for urban economic productivity.

Traffic moves through a network that was designed for a city one-tenth its current size. Electricity supply is supplemented by a distributed system of private generators that have become, effectively, a parallel utility. Waste management operates through informal networks of collectors who know their routes more intimately than any GIS database.

## The Informality Premium

What this case study reveals is not, as earlier development economics assumed, a deficit to be corrected. It is, in many respects, a set of adaptive strategies that formal systems have failed to match on flexibility and local knowledge.

## Scalable Interventions

The most successful infrastructure interventions in Lagos over the past decade have been those that worked with existing informal logics rather than attempting to replace them wholesale — bus rapid transit corridors that formalized existing jitney routes, waste valorization programs that contracted existing collector networks, and market regularization schemes that provided title and utilities without displacing established commercial relationships.
