The record-breaking auction results for artists of African descent at Christie's, Sotheby's, and Phillips over the past five years represent more than a market correction. They signal a fundamental revaluation of the cultural production of the African diaspora — a revaluation that has implications far beyond the art market itself.

Soft power, as the political scientists define it, operates through attraction rather than coercion. A nation or a people that can make their culture, values, and narratives desirable exerts influence that no military or economic instrument can replicate at the same cost.

## The Institutional Infrastructure

What is striking about the current moment is not merely the price appreciation of individual works, but the parallel construction of institutional infrastructure — Black-owned galleries, diaspora-focused auction houses, artist residency networks — that is beginning to replicate, for the first time, the conditions that allowed other cultural traditions to build durable market positions.

## Capital Follows Culture

The lesson for diaspora leaders and institutions is clear: cultural visibility is not merely a soft benefit, but a strategic asset that attracts capital, talent, and partnership. The artists leading this moment understood this intuitively. The institutions supporting them are learning it.
